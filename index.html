Investigating the effect that the mass of salt in water has on its specific heat capacity.

Research question

After an experiment in class, I found I was keen to explore the concept of specific heat capacity further. I thought that the link between the mass of salt dissolved in water and specific heat capacity would be interesting to investigate. Hence, my research question is:

How does the mass of salt dissolved in water affect the specific heat capacity of the solution?

Theory

The specific heat capacity is a constant value existing for each unique material. It is defined by the amount of energy it takes to heat 1 kilogram of that material by 1 kelvin. To calculate the specific heat capacity, one must measure the amount of thermal energy transferred into or out of a material of known mass. Often, this value is used to calculate the amount of energy required to change a materials temperature:

Equation 1

Q = m c Δ T

Where:

  • Q = Energy in J
  • m = Mass of substance in k g
  • c = Specific heat capacity in J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1
  • Δ T = Change in temperature in K or ° C
We can rewrite this equation for specific heat capacity:

Equation 2

c = Q m Δ T

In this experiment, the magnitude of energy transfer is measured as the material cools when combined with another material of known heat capacity. We find the magnitude of this energy transfer by substituting the temperature drop of the known material into equation 1, then substituting that into equation 2. This produces the following:

Equation 3

c s = m w c w Δ T w m s Δ T s

Where:

  • c s = Specific heat capacity of solution in J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1
  • m w = Mass of material of known specific heat capacity in k g
  • c w = Specific heat capacity of known material in J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1
  • Δ T w = Temperature change of known material in K or ° C
  • m s = Mass of salt solution in k g
  • Δ T s = Temperature change of solution in K or ° C

Hypothesis

Equation 3 relates the specific heat capacity to the mass of the solution. The equation states an inverse proportionality between the specific heat capacity of the solution and the mass of salt in the solution. Given this relationship, it is expected that as the mass of salt increases, the specific heat capacity decreases at a decreasing rate. The graph of mass of salt against the inverse of the specific heat capacity will have a positive, linear correlation. Since a mass of 0 salt in the water would be distilled water, the graph is expected to have a y-intercept of 4186 J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1 (Engineering Toolbox n.d.)

Method

Initially, I wanted to pursue an experiment that investigated specific heat capacity by heating solutions rather than cooling them down. In order to achieve this, I decided to place a heating element in the solution along with a thermometer. This method appeared sound to me until I had completed my control trial and began to use salt. I accidentally made bleach by running a current through the sodium chloride solution. I considered indirectly heating the water, however, I decided it is too difficult to heat and insulate water using the equipment in our school lab.

I abandoned that method and decided on this alternative method that uses cooling to determine the specific heat capacity rather than heating.

  1. Note the mass of calorimeter.
  2. Fill the beaker with water so that when the test tube is filled with 25ml of solution and submerged in the water, the water level is above that of the solution.
  3. Place the beaker on the hot plate and heat to above 90°C.
  4. Fill calorimeter with 150ml of distilled water.
  5. Submerge the digital thermometer in the calorimeter and note the temperature.
  6. Dissolve 0.0005kg of salt in 25ml of water.
  7. Transfer solution into test tube and submerge test tube in water. Heat to 90°C exactly.
  8. Once heated, transfer solution to calorimeter quickly and start the digital thermometer.
  9. Watch the thermometer carefully and record its maximum temperature.
  10. Record the combined final weight of solution and water. Discard the combined solution.
  11. Repeat steps 4, 6, 7, 8, 9 for different amounts of salt, including a control trial with 0kg of salt.
SAFETY PRECAUTIONS:
  • The test tube is extremely hot after it is heated to 90°C. Use gloves and take care with the liquid.
  • The hot plate is extremely hot. Stay aware of it and keep hands clear.

Example calculation of specific heat capacity:

Given:

  • m w = 0.001500 kg
  • c w = 4186 J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1
  • Δ T w = 301.6-291.9 K (room temperature subtracted from measured temperature)
  • m s = 0.025 + 0.0005 kg (water mass added to salt mass)
  • Δ T s = 90-28.6 K (measured temperature subtracted from heated temperature)
c s = m w c w Δ T w m s Δ T s = 0.15 ⋅ 4186 ⋅ 9.7 0.02309 ⋅ 61.4 = 4296

Propagation of uncertainty in specific heat capacity

Using equation 3: c s = m w c w Δ T w m s Δ T s , as: a = b c d e f

Uncertainty in specific heat capacity is then:

Δ a a = Δ b b + Δ c c + Δ d d + Δ e e + Δ f f

However, c is the known value of specific heat capacity for water, so it does not need to be considered.

Example propagation for salt mass of 0.0005kg:

Find fractional uncertainty:

0.0001 0.15 + 0.25 28.58 − 18.9 + 0.000002 0.0255 + 0.25 90 − 28.58 = 0.03063321

Convert to absolute and round to a single significant figure:

0.03063321 ⋅ 4196.310053 = 128.55 ⇒ 100 J ⋅ k g − 1 ⋅ K − 1

Analysis

The hypothesis predicted that the graph of mass of salt against the inverse of the specific heat capacity will have a positive, linear correlation. The gradient of the main trendline on the graph proves this prediction. This trendline is valid since it passes through all the error bars. The maximum and minimum lines of best fit, while maintaining the positive, linear correlation, do not pass through the error bars, invalidating them. This is indicative of some random error in the data.

  • Best fit gradient: 0.0142 J ⋅ k g − 2 ⋅ K − 1
  • Min fit gradient: 0.0104 J ⋅ k g − 2 ⋅ K − 1
  • Max fit gradient: 0.0211 J ⋅ k g − 2 ⋅ K − 1
  • Gradient: 0.0142 ± 0.00535 J ⋅ k g − 2 ⋅ K − 1
  • Gradient percentage uncertainty: 37.6%

The gradient has a large uncertainty. This means the precision could be greatly improved. Despite issues with precision, the data does appear to be rather accurate since the points appear consistently close to the best fit gradient. There are two possible anomalies at 0.0025 and 0.003. The former sits below the average trendline shown by other points and the latter sits well above the trendline. Considering the error bars however, both points could reasonably sit on the trendline.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the mass of sodium chloride in a solution is proportional to the inverse of its specific heat capacity.

Evaluation

The design and method of this investigation was suitable for reaching a viable conclusion. However, the experiment experienced some systematic and random error that impacted the accuracy and precision of the data.

Weakness Significance Improvement
The solution in the test tube was not evenly heated since there is a temperature gradient in the beaker of water that is replicated in the test tube. The amount of mixing of solution was not controlled and final temperature of solution was not recorded. The experiment assumes an even temperature distribution across the solution. This issue means that the average temperature over the solution could be significantly above or below the value used (90°C). The conductor of experiment could control the stirring periods and ensure the solution is evenly heated by moving the thermometer around the solution before combining the soution and distilled water in calorimeter.
The solution in the test tube sometimes had a considerable amount of time out of water as it was being transferred to calorimeter. Being out of the water meant that the solution could have cooled down, further invalidating the 90°C value used in calculations. The conductor of the experiment could move the calorimeter and hot plate close together to minimise the time out of water. The conductor could also consider some kind of insulation for the test tube. Alternatively, a second person could measure the temperature of solution before entering the water.
Manufacturing defects or damage to the calorimeters was not examined when conducting multiple trials concurrently. While not visually apparant, I noticed that between trials the values of specefic heat capacity would also vary with the calorimeter I used. This is despite the calorimeters being seemingly identical. This would add some random errors in the data. Do not conduct multiple trials concurrently. Only use one calorimeter.
The calorimeter was cold for the first trial in each session that I conducted this experiment. Some thermal energy would be lost due to heating the calorimeter in the first trial. This would mean the final temperature value may be slightly lower for these trials. Warm the calorimeter with hot water before conducting any trials.
